All hold, no jeopardy

Jannik Sinner’s serve was never broken at Wimbledon. A win-probability needle shows why the final was calmer than it looked: close score ≠ high drama

On the evening of July 12th 2026, Jannik Sinner hit a forehand that Alexander Zverev could not answer. The Italian sank to the Centre Court grass for the second July in a row. He had beaten the German 6-7, 7-6, 6-3, 6-4 to keep the title he won a year before. It was his fifth grand slam and his hundredth match won at the majors. But the applause was a shade cooler this time. Carlos Alcaraz, whose presence had made the 2025 final feel like an occasion, was nowhere near this one. The fortnight will not be filed among the vintage years. Yet the match wore the costume of a thriller: two opening sets settled in tie-breaks, and a champion who lost the first of them.

Sport calls a match "great" long before anyone checks the claim. And the word actually means two different things. One is execution: how well the points were made, the rallies sustained, the ball struck clean. The other is drama: how close the contest was, and how wildly the advantage swung from one man to the other. These two need not go together. A flawless demolition can be beautiful and quite dead. A shank-strewn brawl can be thrilling and ugly. To judge a match honestly, you have to pull the two apart first.

This article scores every final it examines on one match-quality index, out of 100. Five ingredients feed it: how well its points were built, how close it ran, how wildly the lead swung, how long the bodies held, and, over the lot as a multiplier, what was at stake.

The match-quality index
MQI = ( 0.30·Execution + 0.20·Competitiveness + 0.30·Drama + 0.20·Endurance ) × Stakes
Each component is scored 0 to 100; stakes is a multiplier near 1. Drama carries the joint-heaviest weight, alongside execution, and is the least obvious to measure.

Four of the five ingredients are the ordinary currency of match reports. Drama, the swing of advantage, is the hard one to pin down, and doing that honestly is the point of this piece. Here is how: stop watching the players and watch a single needle instead. It shows the probability, recalculated after every point, that the eventual champion will lift the trophy. One rule matters most: assume the two men are otherwise evenly matched, each holding serve at the match's own average. That way the needle starts at even and moves only when the score does, never because of reputation. This is deliberate. A bookmaker would have opened with Mr Sinner a heavy favourite, but that measures the players, not the afternoon. Strip out the skill gap, and the needle registers nothing but what happened on court. A one-sided procession leaves it becalmed. A real comeback sends it plunging toward zero, then hauling back toward one. Add up the size of every move it makes, game by game, small shifts and violent ones alike. That total is drama, now given a number.

Set the needle loose on the 2026 final and, for all the tie-breaks, it hardly moves. Mr Sinner opened at 50%, sank to a low of 28% after he went a set down, and from the middle of the second set never trailed again. Two tie-break sets, and not one change of leader across nearly four hours. On a scale that runs from a routine afternoon to the sport's modern summits, the match earns a drama score of just 49 out of 100. That is one of the lowest scores here, despite the two tie-breaks, and a shade below Mr Sinner's own final a year earlier. The reason lies in the serve. Neither man was broken cheaply. Mr Sinner was not broken at all, and Mr Zverev managed only a single break point in the whole match. So each set simply rode the serve to 6-6 and fell into a tie-break. This is where the needle draws its sharpest distinction: between a match that is close and one that is volatile. Closeness is a small margin on the scoreboard. Volatility is the champion's win probability actually moving, and that only happens when a lead changes hands. Two men holding serve to 6-6 have settled nothing between them, so the needle barely stirs even while the score stays tight. A tie-break reached that way is a coin toss balanced on a stalemate, not a swing of fortune. This match was close. It was not volatile.

A sceptic might ask whether the needle would even recognise a classic. So test it on the ghosts. Show it the 2019 Wimbledon final, where Novak Djokovic saved two championship points against Roger Federer: it drops the eventual winner to an 8% whisper, then drags him back from the dead. Show it the same pair's Roland Garros final of 2025, five and a half hours long, where Mr Alcaraz erased three match points: it sends its winner down to a scarcely believable 5%. The needle is told nothing about reputation. Yet it ranks these two great escapes first and second, and sends each hero to the brink exactly where he stood in real life.

Now widen the lens to every Wimbledon final the charting project has logged, and score each on the whole index, not drama alone. Two decades open out below. Height is the overall score. The size of each circle is the drama score, the measured travel of the needle. The picture makes this article's argument at a glance: size and height often part ways. A wild final is not always a great one. A great one is not always wild.

The table is unkind to received wisdom. The 2019 final tops it at 87: a big circle high on the board, drama and closeness and stamina all at once. Just behind sit two finals memory tends to rank lower: the pristine striking of 2014 and the five-set marathon of 2009. And the 2008 final, the one nearly everyone calls the greatest ever played, rates 72. Fourth in its own tournament, and only a slim margin behind. That is no insult. Its endurance was near-total and its drama among the highest here. But its rallies were only middling in length, and its striking was clean without being the era's cleanest. So on the ledger it settles just below finals built on purer ball-striking or greater length. The index already grants 2008 one of its largest stakes premiums, and it still finishes fourth. That is because stakes measures only what was on the line beforehand, and it is capped. It cannot weigh what a match becomes in memory: the failing light, the two championship points Mr Federer saved, the slow sense of an era turning. The index scores the match. The legend counts more.

Where does Mr Sinner's 2026 fall? At 52, a shade above the median of 48. That puts it in the broad middle of the modern era. Its rally length, unlike every other final here, is estimated rather than charted. Execution is its relative strength: the striking was clean, about three winners for every two unforced errors, and points were rarely given away even though the rallies ran short. But the needle scarcely moved, the match ran only four sets, and nothing hung on a knife-edge. So drama, competitiveness and endurance all sit low. The result is a well-made final, not a monument. Its 2025 predecessor, the first Sinner–Alcaraz Wimbledon final, scores 39, a notch lower still: for all its billing, a four-set win with a poorer winner-to-error count and not a single tie-break. Neither belongs with 2019 or the cleaner classics above them. The gap between a fine final and a great one, on this evidence, is about thirty-five points. And it is made of drama, closeness, and the hours a match demands.

One honesty is owed to the tie-breaks. The measure samples the match once a game. So a whole tie-break, a dozen or more knife-edge points, registers as a single step. Inside those two shoot-outs, the point-by-point odds did swing sharply. A spectator who found them gripping was not wrong. The game-by-game reading simply cannot see that far in. But it does capture the question that decides the score: who would win the match. On that, Mr Sinner was seldom in real doubt.

None of this dims Mr Sinner's fortnight; it merely files it correctly. He kept the sport's oldest title, reached his fifth major, and won his hundredth match at the grand slams. Those are the marks of a player quietly bending an era around himself. The numbers ask only that the day be remembered for the right reason. The record books will keep this final; the pulse never will

How the index is built

Every final in this article is scored out of 100 by one match-quality index: four ingredients measured on court, and a fifth applied over the top as a multiplier.

The match-quality index
MQI = ( 0.30·Execution + 0.20·Competitiveness + 0.30·Drama + 0.20·Endurance ) × Stakes
Execution = 100 × [ ½·clamp( (R̄ − 2.8) ÷ 2.5 ) + ½·clamp( (w − 0.42) ÷ 0.26 ) ]
R̄ is the mean rally length in shots. w is the winners’ share of winners plus unforced errors: w = W ÷ (W + UE). Each half is capped to the 0-to-1 range. So execution rewards long rallies and clean, winner-heavy striking equally. The other three on-court components use their own fixed scales, described below. Why these weights? 0.3 on execution and drama, 0.2 on competitiveness and endurance. Match quality has no external truth to check them against, so they are a considered judgement, not a fit. Drama and execution simply carry the most weight. And the broad order holds under almost any reasonable reweighting: the 2019 epic and the marathons stay high, the blowouts stay low. Only the middle of the table moves.
Execution · scored 0 to 100
How the points were made and finished. It combines two measured things, in equal part: the mean rally length (how long points were sustained, stroke by stroke) and the balance of winners to unforced errors (how cleanly those points ended). Long, cleanly struck finals score high. Short or error-strewn ones score low. In figures: Execution = 100 × [ ½(mean rally − 2.8)÷2.5 + ½(w − 0.42)÷0.26 ], each half held to the 0-to-100 range. The 2014 final, long-rallied and clean, tops this scale. The error-strewn 2017 final sits at the bottom. The 2026 final is the one exception in the whole table: it was not charted shot by shot, so its rally length is estimated rather than counted, and its execution and index are marked accordingly.
Competitiveness · scored 0 to 100
How close the contest ran: the number of sets and tie-breaks, the margin of the decisive set, and how near the loser came to winning. A straight-sets rout scores low; a five-set final decided in the last game scores high.
Drama · scored 0 to 100
The heart of the method. After every point, recompute the probability that the eventual champion will win. Read that line once per game. Sum the size of every move it makes, game by game: its total distance travelled. A procession barely moves it. A real comeback sends it plunging, then hauling back. That total sits on a scale where a routine final scores near 40 and the 2019 Wimbledon epic anchors the top near 95. Two choices shape it. First, the players are set to equal strength, each serving at the match average, so the line follows the score rather than the odds; this measures drama, not who was likelier to win. Second, it is sampled once a game, which keeps the arithmetic clean but under-counts the tension inside a tie-break, itself a dozen knife-edge points read as one step. The 2026 final scores 49, low despite its two tie-breaks; 2008 scores 68; 2019 anchors the ceiling at 95.
Endurance · scored 0 to 100
The physical toll: the length of the match and the number of games and points played. Longer, heavier finals score higher.
Stakes · a multiplier, not a score
The only ingredient not read from the court. It captures what the match meant: the occasion, the rivalry, and what was on the line, whether a ranking, a streak or a place in history. Rather than a 0-to-100 score, it is applied as a multiplier near 1, running roughly 1.00 to 1.15. So it can lift the on-court total by at most a tenth or so. The values are fixed in advance from the public record: the ranking, streak or milestone genuinely at stake, not tuned to how the match turned out. Another analyst might shade them by a point or two, which is exactly why the reach is capped so tightly, so such disagreement can move a final's total by only a point or so. The largest premiums here go to 2008, 2011 and 2023 (about 1.11), each a marquee rivalry final with the No. 1 ranking or a milestone in play. The 2026 final draws 1.06, with Mr Alcaraz absent and the mood a shade cooler.
